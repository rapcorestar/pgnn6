# pgnn — six: read before changing the room

This is a digital installation, judged as an exhibition piece. It is not an interactive album, a game, a mystery, or a catalogue of clickable objects.

Its only job is to recreate this feeling:

> You have entered a place that somehow already knows you.

The apartment is a small, Soviet-era Eastern European rental in winter. It was built in the early 1980s, poorly renovated in the late 2000s, and occupied by one person since around 2019. Nothing was selected as décor; it accumulated. The occupant was here five minutes ago, not decades ago.

## Non-negotiables

- No menus, buttons, prompts, puzzles, collectables, progress, achievements, or explanations.
- Attention leads to interaction. Most of the room should never respond; when a detail has earned attention, a quiet click can be a physical commitment to move closer or touch it.
- Every object needs a private history. If it has no emotional reason to be present, remove it.
- Never make an animation announce itself. Movement must feel incidental: a curtain breathing, steam thinning, dust drifting.
- Sound is quiet life, not a soundtrack button. Music enters as a discovery, never with a title or progress bar.
- The room offers evidence, never a narrative answer.
- Browser persistence should alter the room slowly and ambiguously. It forgets itself rather than rewarding completion.

## Lighting and palette

It is approximately 17:40 in January. The exterior is blue-hour winter; the interior has a tired tungsten lamp and a little CRT glow. Keep roughly 70% of the frame dark. Concrete grey, dust beige, faded wood, dirty white, deep plant green, and winter blue make the world. Warm orange is the only allowed accent.

## Decision test

Before adding anything, ask: does it make the visitor remember *an apartment*, as if from a dream? If not, it does not belong in `pgnn — six`.
